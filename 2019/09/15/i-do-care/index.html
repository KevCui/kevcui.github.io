I Don't Care?


Photo by Mitch Lensink

Let’s start with your imagination: in a team discussion, someone purposes a solution to solve a certain problem. However, another person expresses a strong opinion against that solution. The discussion is heating up with arguments on two sides back and forth. You are sitting there in your zone silently and listening to the whole conversation insouciantly.

Suddenly, someone asks you, “What’s your opinion?”

You replied without hesitation, “I don’t care.”

…

I don’t care.

“I don’t care” literately can be a valid answer to almost any questions. At some point, “I don’t care” may tell the true feeling and real attitude.

However, it’s a powerful conversation terminator, which not only closes the ongoing conversation but also slaps the door to refuse further conversations. Sometimes when using “I don’t care”, it can be harmful to create isolations from your colleagues.

There can be different reasons behind the conclusion “I don’t care”. To figure out what causes “I don’t care” is important to understand the context of the problem. How about giving more explicit answer using the alternative “I do care if….”

I do care if…

In “I do care if…", the if part is the condition which acts as the trigger. It explains why “I don’t care”, it’s because “if” condition is not well fulfilled. This can open a deeper conversation to think towards the root cause. For example:

  • I realize the context that we were discussing the wrong problem, “I do care if it’s really a critical problem we are discussing, however, it’s not."

  • I know that my inputs are useless for decision making (yes, it can happen; and yes, it sucks), “I do care if my voice had been heard before the decision was made."

  • I have no ideas and I need time to think, “I do care if I want to contribute some thoughts in this meeting. However, I have none and I need time to think."

As you can see, “I don’t care” doesn’t always mean the topic is lack of my interest. It can be many unsaying reasons. If you would like to help others understand the context of why “I don’t care”, “I do care if…” might be a good alternative.

Furthermore, the conversation and discussion itself is not important in many cases. The important thing is the reflected, unnoticed, harmful work culture during the discussion. And the person who says “I don’t care” may be the one who already realized this harmful work culture before others do. If you want to know more about the context from that person, you can also use “I do care if…” as an open question “In which condition, you would care about it?".

Overall, do care when being present.
